Using a sample to estimate the population mean

Jeu 25 mars 2021
Category: Statistics

In this article we saw that the sampling distribution of the mean is normally distributed around the population mean. We use this property to estimate the population mean. We will see now how we can do and visualize this.

We will start where we left off and re-create a nice sampling distribution of means. I will start by creating a population of size population_size for which I measured a variable Value, that happens to follow (again) a Lomax (or Pareto II) distribution.

I will then immediatly collect number_of_samples samples ; of size sample_size ; and write down their means (and standard deviations, we will need them later).

def collect_samples(population_size, sample_size, number_of_samples):
    population = (np.random.pareto(3, population_size) + 1) * 2
    samples = [np.random.choice(population, sample_size, replace=False) 
               for i in range(0, number_of_samples)]
    
    sample_means = [np.mean(s) for s in samples]
    sample_stds = [np.std(s) for s in samples]

    print("""
Population mean: {:.3f}
""".format(population.mean()))

    return population.mean(), population.std(), sample_means, sample_stds

Let us create a population with 100,000 observations, collect 10,000 samples of 1,000 observations each.

def plot_sample_means(
    sample_means, population_mean, xmin=None, xmax=None, figsize=(10, 5)):
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=figsize)
    
    # the weights are used to display percentages of samples
    n = len(sample_means)
    weights_pc = np.array([1.]*n) / n
    
    # adding the sample means histogram
    bins_n, bins_val, rectangles = ax.hist(
        sample_means, bins=100, weights=weights_pc, color="tab:blue"
    )

    # adding the population mean
    vline = ax.axvline(
        population_mean, color="black", linewidth=1, linestyle= "dashed"
    )
    
    # setting x limits
    if xmin is None: xmin = ax.get_xlim()[0]
    if xmax is None: xmax = ax.get_xlim()[1]
    ax.set_xlim((xmin, xmax))

    # formatting y ticks
    y_ticks_loc = ax.get_yticks().tolist()
    ax.yaxis.set_major_locator(mticker.FixedLocator(y_ticks_loc))
    ax.set_yticklabels(["{:,.1%}".format(y) for y in y_ticks_loc])

    # axis labels
    ax.set_xlabel("Sample means", fontsize=12)
    ax.set_ylabel("Percentage of samples", fontsize=12)

    return fig, ax, bins_n, bins_val, rectangles

Good, our sampling distribution of means looks normally distributed - bell shaped - and centered around the population mean (it is not perfectly normally distributed, but we will come back to that later).

Normal Distribution of the sampling distribution of means¶

A normal distribution relies (by definition) on 2 parameters: its mean and its standard deviation. Let us look into those.

The mean of the sampling distribution of means¶

The good news is that the mean of our sample means (sdom_mean with sdom being sampling distribution of means in short) is the population mean. Indeed a normal distribution is centered around its own mean and we already know the sampling distribution of means is centered around the population mean.

In [6]:
def compare_sdom_mean_and_population_mean(
    sample_means, population_mean):
    print("""
Population mean: {:.3f}
Mean of the sample means: {:.3f}
""".format(population_mean, np.mean(sample_means)))
    return np.mean(sample_means)

The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of means¶

What about the standard deviations of our sample means? It happends that the standard deviations of sample means (also called SEM or standard error of the mean, here the sem variable) is equal to the population standard deviation (population_std) divided by the square root of the sample_size.

This can be demonstrated mathematically, but we will just test it here.

In [8]:
def compare_sem_and_adjusted_population_std(
    size_of_each_sample, sample_means, population_std):
    adjusted_population_std = population_std / (size_of_each_sample**(1/2))
    
    text = """
Population mean divided by the square root of the sample size: {:.3f}
Standard deviation computed from the sample means: {:.3f}
"""
    
    print(text.format(adjusted_population_std ,np.std(sample_means)))
    return np.std(sample_means)

The 95,45% rule¶

Being normally distributed comes with some usefull properties. One of which states that 95.45% of normally distributed values lay within the 2 first standard deviations around the mean.

The "2" is called a z-value (more on this here). It changes depending of the percentage of values we want to find. (A z-value of "1" is 68.27%, a z-value of "3" is 99.73%.)

Applied to our situation, it means that 95.45% of our sample means lay within the 2 first standard error of the mean around the population mean.

Let us color the sample means within that range and see what it gives. The central bin will be in orange and all the bins in the range will be in light orange.

In [10]:
def add_proba_range_to_plot(
    fig, ax, bins_n, bins_val, rectangles, selected_mean, selected_range):
    # finding the bin of our selected value
    central_bin = bins_val[bins_val < selected_mean].max()
    central_index = np.where(bins_val == central_bin)[0][0]

    # finding the bottom bin of our range
    lower_mean = selected_mean - selected_range
    lower_bin = bins_val[bins_val < (lower_mean)].max()
    lower_index = np.where(bins_val == lower_bin)[0][0]

    # finding the top bin of our range
    upper_mean = selected_mean + selected_range    
    upper_bin = bins_val[bins_val < (upper_mean)].max()
    upper_index = np.where(bins_val == upper_bin)[0][0]
    
    for i in range(lower_index, upper_index):
        rectangles[i].set_color("navajowhite")

    rectangles[central_index].set_color("orange")
    
    return fig, ax

def test_probability_interval(sample_means, population_mean, sem):
    n_samples = len(sample_means)
    n_true = len([
        s for s in sample_means \
        if (s < (population_mean + 2.*sem)) \
        and (s > (population_mean - 2.*sem))])

    text = """Percentage of samples in range: {:.2%}"""

    print(text.format(n_true / n_samples))

So what now ?¶

When I have collected only one sample, how do I estimate the population_mean?

The first best guess we can do is to use the single sample mean to estimate the population_mean. And that is not terrible, but using what we know of the sampling distribution of mean, we can do better.

Our sample is somewhere in the bell shape, but we do not know where exactly. We are going to address that incertainty by building an interval around our single sample mean in which we believe the population mean is likely to be.

We are going to build a confidence interval defined as follow:

95.45% of our confidence intervals must include the population mean.

Let us try and reverse the formula of the 95.45% probability interval to get our 95.45% confidence interval. The closest thing we have from the population_mean is our single_sample_mean, so we will start with that one. Our confidence interval works if 95.45% of our confidence intervals include the population mean.

We will start by creating confidence intervals around 2 selected samples: one in the middle of the right side, the other one at the extreme right.

Here we see that the population mean is NOT inside the confidence interval. So it does not work everytime.

When we have a single sample, we cannot be sure that the population mean is inside the confidence interval. The best we can do (and are doing) is to defined a method that would work for 95.45% of the confidence intervals. In order to see if our method of building confidence intervals works, we need to take all of our samples, build their individual confidence interval and count how many of those include the actual population mean.

Let us say we know the standard error of the mean, the formula we want to try is:

95.45% confidence interval = sample mean +/- (2x standard error of the mean)

Let us do that now.

In [16]:
def test_confidence_interval_know_SEM(sample_means):
    pop_mean = np.mean(sample_means)
    sem = np.std(sample_means)
    n_samples = len(sample_means)
    n_true = 0
    
    for i in range(0, n_samples):
        if (pop_mean < (sample_means[i] + 2.*sem)) \
            and (pop_mean > (sample_means[i] - 2.*sem)):
            n_true += 1
        
    text = """
Percentage of confidence intervals that include the population 
mean (or mean of sampling distribution of mean): {:.2%}
"""

    print(text.format(n_true / n_samples))

Alright, using a the sample mean and 2x standard error of the mean gives us a 95.45% confidence interval. In practive we don't know the standard error of the mean, so we need to estimate it from our sample in order to build the confidence interval.

Could we estimate the standard error of the mean with the sample standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample_size?

With estimated standard error of the mean:

estimated SEM = sample standard deviation / sqrt(sample size)
95.45% confidence interval = sample mean +/- (2x estimated SEM)

Let us try this out.

In [18]:
def test_confidence_interval_unknow_SEM(
    sample_means, sample_stds, sample_size, score=2.):
    pop_mean = np.mean(sample_means)
    n_samples = len(sample_means)
    n_true = 0
    
    for i in range(0, n_samples):
        est_sem = sample_stds[i] / (sample_size ** (1/2))
        if (pop_mean < (sample_means[i] + score * est_sem)) \
            and (pop_mean > (sample_means[i] - score * est_sem)):
            n_true += 1
        
    text = """
Percentage of confidence intervals including the population 
mean (or mean of sampling distribution of mean): {:.2%}
"""

    print(text.format(n_true / n_samples))

Being more confident of confidence intervals¶

There are several elements we purposely ignored. Our percentages were close but never quite the 95.45% we were looking for. And the gap increased. So what happended?

Our sampling distribution of means is not perfectly smooth. That is because we used "only" 10,000 samples. With more samples, our distribution would be smoother, and the difference smaller.

In addition, our original population distribution was strongly asymmetric, heavy tail on the left and long tail on the right. This still reflects in the sampling distribution of means. We see it when comparing the left and right side at the top and on the tails.

def add_asymmetry_to_plot(
    fig, ax, bins_n, bins_val, rectangles, sdom_mean, sdom_std):
    formatting = {
        "linewidth": 1, "edgecolor":"none", "facecolor": "k", "alpha": 0.2}
    min_y, max_y = ax.get_ylim()
    
    # top
    top_x_delta = 0.1
    top_x_min = sdom_mean - (top_x_delta / 2)
    top_y_delta = 0.01
    top_y_min = max_y - top_y_delta
    top_rectangle = mpatches.Rectangle(
        (top_x_min, top_y_min), top_x_delta, top_y_delta, **formatting
    )

    # bottom (b)
    b_x_delta = 0.2
    b_y_delta = 0.002
    b_y_min = min_y

    # bottom right (br)
    br_x_min = sdom_mean + (2*sdom_std) 
    br_rectangle = mpatches.Rectangle(
        (br_x_min, b_y_min), b_x_delta, b_y_delta, **formatting
    )

    # bottom left (bl)
    bl_x_min = sdom_mean - (2*sdom_std) - b_x_delta
    bl_rectangle = mpatches.Rectangle(
        (bl_x_min, b_y_min), b_x_delta, b_y_delta, **formatting
    )
    
    # adding to plot
    ax.add_patch(top_rectangle)
    ax.add_patch(br_rectangle)
    ax.add_patch(bl_rectangle)
    
    return fig, ax

Those differences are pulling us away from a normal distribution, therefor impacting the precision of our estimations.

In practice, scientists and engineers will change the underlying values (following a Lomax in our case): trimming outliers and transforming the distribution to fit a normal one. This becomes especially necessary with smaller samples.

Small Samples¶

While we used a big sample size, a lot of the science cannot afford that syze of samples. So how does a small sample affects our confidence interval?

Well, no surprises here, it impacts again the precision of our estimations.

In practice, scientists and engineers will replace the z-value by a t-value. T-values are very close from z-values for large samples (30 < x < 10% population) but differ quite a bit for smaller ones. While z-values are taken from a standard normal distribution (mean = 0 and standard deviation = 1), t-values are taken from the student distribution with (sample_size - 1) degrees of freedom. That is probably a bit criptic, so we can remember it like that: t-values are adjusting for the impact of the sample size.

In our example, the t-value would be around 2.002508, so very much close to our 2.000000, thanks to our large samples.
