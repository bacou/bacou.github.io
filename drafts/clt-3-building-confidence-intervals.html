Using a sample to estimate the population mean (CLT 3)

21/06/2021
Catégorie : statistiques

In this article we saw that the sampling distribution of the mean is normally distributed around the population mean, as established by the Central Limit Theorem. We use this property to estimate the population mean using only a single sample. We will see now how we can do and visualize this estimation.

def generate_rand_lomax(n):
    a, m = 3., 2.  # shape and mode
    s = (np.random.pareto(a, n) + 1) * m  # np.random.pareto makes a Lomax
    return s

def plot_population_distribution(data):
    # plotting the distribution and its mean
    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 6))

    # the weights are used to display percentages of samples
    n = len(data)
    weights_pc = np.array([1.]*n) / n

    # adding the histogram

    ax.axvline(

    ax.set_ylabel("Percentage of observations", fontsize=12)
    ax.set_xlabel("Value", fontsize=12)
    ax.legend(loc="upper right")

    return fig

def compute_sample_means(
    population_data, sample_size, number_of_samples):

    means = list()
    stds = list()

    for i in range(0, number_of_samples):
        sample = np.random.choice(population_data, sample_size, replace=False)
        means.append(np.mean(sample))
        stds.append(np.std(sample, ddof=1))
    
    return means, stds

Nothing new here, our sampling distribution of means is normally distributed - bell-shaped - and centered around the population mean. Before trying any estimation of the population mean, let us go over some usefull things.

Normal distribution of the sampling distribution of means¶

A normal distribution relies (by definition) on 2 parameters: its mean and its standard deviation. Let us look into those for our sampling distribution of means and how they relate to their population counterparts.

The mean of the sampling distribution of means¶

The mean is straightforward. We clearly established that the mean of our sampling distribution of means is the population mean.

What about the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of means?¶

It happens that the standard deviations of sample means is equal to the population standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size. It is also called standard error of the mean or SEM, so we will call it sem in the code.

This can be demonstrated mathematically, but we will just test it here.

Note: We currently make no link between the sample standard deviations and the SEM, the 1st one is the standard deviation of each sample, while the 2nd one is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution of means.

In [11]:
def compare_sem_and_population_std(
    population_std, sample_size, sample_means):
    
    adj_population_std = population_std / (sample_size**(1/2))
    sem = np.std(sample_means)
    
    print("""
    Population mean divided by the square root of the sample size: {:.4f}
    Standard error of the mean: {:.4f}
    Difference: {:.4f}
    """.format(
        adj_population_std,
        sem,
        adj_population_std-sem)
    )

Nice, with no difference (or almost no difference), we can say that the standard deviations of sample means is equal to the population standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size. Now we can go back to our estimation exercise.

Estimating the population mean¶

Point estimation¶

The first estimation of the population mean we can do is to directly use the mean of our unique sample. It is called a point estimation. Of course the best case would be to find the mean of our sampling distribution of means but in practice we can rarely afford to take multiple samples. Using the fact that the sampling distribution of means is normally distributed, we can do better than a point estimation.

Building an interval¶

Using a single sample and the fact that the sampling distribution of means is normally distributed, we can build a value interval for the population mean. That brings us to a usefull property of all normal distributions: 95.45% of the (normally distributed) values lay within the 2 first standard deviations around the mean.

The "2" is called a z-value (more on this here). It changes depending on the percentage of values we want to find. (A z-value of "1" gives 68.27%, a z-value of "3" gives 99.73%.) Applied to our situation, it means that 95.45% of our sample means lay within the 2 first standard error of the mean around the mean of the sample means (equal to the population mean).

Let us color all the means within that range and see what we get. The central bin will be in orange and all the bins inside the range will be colored in light orange.

def plot_sample_means_with_range(
    sample_means, selected_center, selected_range, 
    bins=100, figsize=(10, 6), xlim=None, ylim=None):

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=figsize)

    # distribution histogram
    bins_n, bins_val, rectangles = ax.hist(sample_means, bins)

    # distribution mean
    mean_line = ax.axvline(
        np.mean(sample_means), color="g", linestyle="dashed", linewidth=1,
        label="Mean of sample means = {:.3f}".format(np.mean(sample_means)))
    
    # finding the bin of our selected center
    central_bin = bins_val[bins_val < selected_center].max()
    central_index = np.where(bins_val == central_bin)[0][0]

    # finding the bottom bin of our range
    lower_mean = selected_center - selected_range
    lower_bin = bins_val[bins_val < (lower_mean)].max()
    lower_index = np.where(bins_val == lower_bin)[0][0]

    # finding the top bin of our range
    upper_mean = selected_center + selected_range    
    upper_bin = bins_val[bins_val < (upper_mean)].max()
    upper_index = np.where(bins_val == upper_bin)[0][0]
    
    # coloring
    for i in range(lower_index, upper_index):
        rectangles[i].set_color("navajowhite")
    rectangles[central_index].set_color("orange")
    
    # formatting 
    if xlim is not None:
        ax.set_xlim(xlim)
    ax.set_xlabel("Average value of samples", fontsize=12)
    if ylim is not None:
        ax.set_ylim(ylim)
    ax.set_ylabel("Number of samples", fontsize=12)
    ax.legend(loc="upper right")

    return fig

def test_probability_interval(sample_means):
    sdom_mean = np.mean(sample_means)
    sem = np.std(sample_means)
    
    n_samples = len(sample_means)
    n_true = len([
        s for s in sample_means \
        if (s <= (sdom_mean + 2.*sem)) \
        and (s >= (sdom_mean - 2.*sem))])

    print("""
    Percentage of samples in range: {:.2%}
    """.format(n_true / n_samples))

How does that help estimating the population mean?¶

Using an interval instead of a point estimation¶

With a unique sample mean, we have a point estimation of the population mean. This has a strong limitation: it is likely this point estimation is not strictly equal to the population mean, and we don't know how far we are. We could be anywhere inside the sampling distribution of means. In order to get a better estimation of where the population mean truly lies, we want to create a value interval around that sample mean where we believe it is likely to find the said population mean.

The idea is the following, let us say we have a sample of 5 observations for which we measured the size. The sample mean ends up being 180cm. Talking about humans, the tallest ones could be around 2m and the smallest ones around 150cm, so adding 1 observation to our sample would not drastically change its mean. In comparison, talking about trees, the tallest ones could easily go up to 50m, massively impacting our mean of 180cm ! This is why a value interval helps us get a better idea of what we are talking about.

Back to our estimation of the population mean¶

So, with a single sample, we cannot be 100% sure that the population mean is in the vicinity. Each individual sample will have its own value interval and the sample mean could be an oultier, with a mean quite far from the population mean (e.g. a sample in the blue parts of the previous graph). For that reason, we need to create an interval that will work for most of the samples. This interval is called a confidence interval and is defined as follow:

95.45% of (all) confidence intervals include the population mean.

Our confidence interval works if 95.45% of the confidence intervals built around each sample mean include the population mean.

To build that interval, we are going to copy what we did with the probability interval. Starting from the probability interval formula, we replace the population mean by the single sample mean. For now, let us say we know the standard error of the mean, so the formula we want to try is:

95.45% confidence interval = sample mean +/- (2x standard error of the mean)

In order to visualize that, we will start by creating a confidence interval around a first sample mean selected in the middle of the right side of the sampling distribution of mean.

95.45% of our confidence intervals include the population mean.

Let us try and reverse the formula of the 95.45% probability interval to get our 95.45% confidence interval. The closest thing we have to the population_mean is our single_sample_mean, so we will start with that one. Our confidence interval works if 95.45% of our confidence intervals include the population mean.

def test_confidence_interval_know_SEM(sample_means):
    sdom_mean = np.mean(sample_means)
    sem = np.std(sample_means)
    
    n_samples = len(sample_means)
    n_true = 0
    
    n_true = len([
        1 for i in range(0, n_samples) \
        if (sdom_mean < (sample_means[i] + 2.*sem)) \
        and (sdom_mean > (sample_means[i] - 2.*sem))])
    
    print("""
    Percentage of confidence intervals that include the population 
    mean (or mean of sampling distribution of mean): {:.2%}
    """.format(n_true / n_samples))

Alright, so approximatively 95.45% of our intervals (sample mean +/- 2* standard error of the mean) include the population mean. In practive we usually don't know the standard error of the mean, so we need to estimate it from our sample in order to build an actual confidence interval.

Estimating the standard error of the mean¶

Can we estimate the standard error of the mean with the sample standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size?

That would give us:

Estimated SEM = (sample standard deviation) / (square root of sample size)

95.45% confidence interval = (sample mean) +/- (2x estimated SEM)

Let us try this out.

Some samples will have a small standard deviation while others will have a bigger one. Lets compute how many of those ranges include the population mean in total.

In [25]:
def test_confidence_interval_unknow_SEM(
    sample_means, sample_stds, sample_size, score=2.):
    
    population_mean = np.mean(sample_means)
    
    n_true = 0
    for i in range(0, len(sample_means)):
        estimated_sem = sample_stds[i] / (sample_size ** (1/2))
        if (population_mean <= (sample_means[i] + score * estimated_sem)) \
            and (population_mean >= (sample_means[i] - score * estimated_sem)):
            n_true += 1
        
    print("""
    Percentage of confidence intervals including the population 
    mean (or mean of sampling distribution of mean): {:.2%}
    """.format(n_true / len(sample_means)))

Being more confident of confidence intervals¶

We already know that this estimation would change depending on how much we respect the conditions established by the central limit theorem. But there is more: by estimating the SEM - and not using the actual one which is unknown most of the times - we added some more approximation. Let us see how it impacts small samples.
